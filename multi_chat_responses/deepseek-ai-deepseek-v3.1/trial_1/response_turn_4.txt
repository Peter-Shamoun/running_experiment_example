The stored energy in the bands (~127 J) is the *input* energy. The kinetic energy of the projectile is the *output* energy. The ratio of these two values is your efficiency, and it's what you will maximize through meticulous design and tuning.

### 1. Energy Transfer Efficiency (η)

Not all 127 joules of elastic potential energy will be transferred to the projectile. Energy is lost through several mechanisms:

*   **Internal Friction (Hysteresis):** The rubber bands themselves heat up during the rapid contraction, losing a portion of the stored energy. This is often 10-20% loss for latex.
*   **Kinetic Energy of the Bands:** The bands themselves have mass and are accelerated, consuming some energy.
*   **Friction in the System:** Air resistance on the bands, friction in the pouch, etc.
*   **Incomplete Transfer:** The bands may not fully relax to their original length by the time the projectile leaves the pouch.

For a well-designed and tuned slingshot, a realistic and excellent efficiency value is **η = 70%**.

**Therefore, the kinetic energy transferred to the projectile is:**
`KE_projectile = η * E_stored = 0.70 * 127 J ≈ 88.9 Joules`

This is a very significant amount of energy for a 5.5g object and is the key to your maximum range.

### 2. Theoretical Launch Velocity (v)

We calculate the launch velocity from the kinetic energy equation:

**KE = ½ m v²**

Where:
*   `KE` = Kinetic Energy (88.9 J)
*   `m` = mass of the projectile (5.5 g = **0.0055 kg**)
*   `v` = launch velocity (what we need to find)

**Solve for v:**
1.  `½ m v² = KE`
2.  `v² = (2 * KE) / m`
3.  `v = √( (2 * KE) / m )`

`v = √( (2 * 88.9 J) / 0.0055 kg )`
