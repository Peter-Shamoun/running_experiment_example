### Analysis of Upgrading to 1/4" Surgical Tubing

This upgrade fundamentally changes the system from a high-performance handheld device to a piece of equipment that likely requires mechanical assistance to cock and a very serious safety protocol.

#### 1. Force and Energy Calculations

The energy stored in an elastic material is proportional to its cross-sectional area. Doubling the diameter increases the area by a factor of four.

*   **Assumptions:** Same unstretched length (`L₀ = 9 in`), same draw length (`x = 30 in`).
*   **Estimated Spring Constant (`k`):** If 3/16" tubing is ~2.5 lbf/in, then 1/4" tubing is roughly:
    `k_1/4" ≈ k_3/16" * (D_1/4" / D_3/16")² = 2.5 lbf/in * (0.25 / 0.1875)² ≈ 2.5 * (1.333)² ≈ 2.5 * 1.78 ≈ 4.45 lbf/in`
    *(A more accurate empirical value for high-quality 1/4" tubing is often closer to **5.0 - 5.5 lbf/in**)*. We will use **5.0 lbf/in** for this analysis.

*   **Peak Draw Force:**
    `F = k * x = 5.0 lbf/in * 30 in = **150 lbf** (667 N)`.

*   **Stored Energy:**
    `E_stored = ½ * k * x² = ½ * 5.0 lbf/in * (30 in)² = ½ * 5.0 * 900 = **2,250 in-lbf**`.
    Converting to Joules: `2,250 in-lbf * 0.113 J/in-lbf ≈ **254 Joules**`.

**This is double the stored energy of the 3/16" design.** This is a massive amount of energy to be stored in a handheld device.

#### 2. Efficiency and Projectile Energy

With thicker tubing and higher forces, efficiency typically **decreases** due to increased hysteresis (internal friction/heat loss in the rubber) and the greater proportion of energy used to accelerate the heavier bands themselves.

*   **Estimated Efficiency (η):** Let's be optimistic and assume excellent design mitigates losses. **η ≈ 60%** (down from 70%).
*   **Kinetic Energy Output:**
    `KE_out = η * E_stored = 0.60 * 254 J ≈ **152.4 Joules**`.

*   **Theoretical Launch Velocity (5.5g projectile):**
    `v = √( (2 * KE) / m ) = √( (2 * 152.4 J) / 0.0055 kg ) = √(304.8 / 0.0055) = √(55,418) ≈ **235 m/s (526 mph)**`.

This velocity is supersonic (the speed of sound is ~343 m/s). While the steel bearing could theoretically reach this speed in the pouch, air resistance will be immense and will cause significant deceleration immediately after launch.

### Critical Engineering & Safety Implications

Pursuing this path requires acknowledging and addressing these extreme factors:

**1. Human Factors: Cocking the Device**
A **150 lbf (68 kg-force)** draw force is not humanly possible to hold with one hand and pull with the other in a traditional slingshot grip. This force is equivalent to holding up a full-grown adult.
*   **Solution:** The device would require a **mechanical cocking aid**. This could be:
    *   A **winch and pulley system** integrated into the frame.
    *   A **lever arm** that provides a mechanical advantage.
    *   This fundamentally changes the design from a simple handheld slingshot to a mounted ballista.

**2. Frame and Component Strength**
The forces on the frame and attachments double. Your previous safety factor of ~4 is now reduced to ~2.
*   **Solution:** The frame must be significantly reinforced. A 1" OD aluminum tube with a **0.125" (1/8") wall thickness** would be a minimum recommendation. The band attachment points (e.g., screws, clamps) must be equally over-engineered.

**3. Pouch and Release Mechanism**
The pouch must withstand immense force without stretching or tearing. The release must be flawless; any snag will cause a catastrophic failure.
*   **Solution:** Use a reinforced, non-stretch pouch (e.g., layered leather with a Kydex liner). The release technique becomes even more critical.

**4. Safety Protocol**
The energy stored (254 J) is in the territory of a powerful air rifle. A failure or accidental release could cause severe injury.
*   **Solutions:**
    *   **Integrated Safety:** Design a mechanical safety lock that prevents accidental release once cocked.
    *   **Cocking Aid as Safety:** The cocking mechanism itself can act as a safety.
    *   **Testing Enclosure:** All testing must be done behind a protective barrier rated for high-energy impacts.
