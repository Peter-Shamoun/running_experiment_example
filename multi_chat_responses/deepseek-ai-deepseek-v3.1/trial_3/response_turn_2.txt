### **3. Engineering Rationale & Basic Stress Considerations**

For your analysis, focus on the most highly stressed member: the **launch arm**.

*   **Loading Scenario:** The arm is a cantilever beam in bending. The primary load is the centrifugal force of the projectile (and sabot/sling) at the moment of peak angular velocity, just before release.
*   **Simplified Stress Calculation (for presentation):**
    1.  **Estimate Peak Force:** Use physics to find the centripetal acceleration. For example: If the projectile mass `m` is 0.15 kg (lacrosse ball), the arm radius `r` is 1.5 m, and the estimated tip velocity `v` is 15 m/s, then the force `F` at the tip is `F = (m * v²) / r`.
        *   `F = (0.15 kg * (15 m/s)²) / 1.5 m = 22.5 N`
    2.  **Calculate Bending Moment:** `M = F * r` (for a point load at the end).
        *   `M = 22.5 N * 1.5 m = 33.75 N·m`
    3.  **Check Stress:** For a rectangular arm cross-section (e.g., 2"x1" bar), calculate the bending stress `σ = M * y / I`, where `y` is the distance to the neutral axis and `I` is the area moment of inertia.
        *   For a 2"x1" bar (0.0508m x 0.0254m), `I = (width * height³) / 12 = (0.0254 * 0.0508³) / 12 = 2.77e-7 m⁴`
        *   `y = height / 2 = 0.0254 m`
        *   `σ = (33.75 N·m * 0.0254 m) / 2.77e-7 m⁴ ≈ 3.1 MPa`
*   **Analysis:** The calculated bending stress (~3.1 MPa) is **orders of magnitude lower** than the yield strength of Aluminum 6061-T6 (~290 MPa). Therefore, the arm has a very high factor of safety.
    *   **Presentation Point:** This isn't over-engineering; it's ensuring **absolute rigidity**. Any deflection in the arm would change the release point and trajectory, destroying **precision**. The high stiffness of the chosen material and cross-section guarantees repeatable performance. The safety factor also accounts for dynamic loads and shock, aligning perfectly with the contest's emphasis on **safety and control**.
