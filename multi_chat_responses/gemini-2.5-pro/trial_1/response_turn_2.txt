### Slingshot Frame Design Recommendations

A long-draw setup puts significant torque on your wrist and requires a very robust, ergonomic frame. The goal is to create a frame that is safe, comfortable to hold under high tension, and allows the bands to operate without interference.

#### 1. Frame Material & Thickness
This is the most critical choice for safety. A frame failure at full draw with powerful bands is extremely dangerous.

*   **Primary Recommendation: Baltic Birch Plywood**
    *   **Why:** It's an engineered wood product with cross-laminated plies, making it incredibly strong and resistant to splitting along a grain line. It's affordable, easy to work with, and the standard for high-quality handmade wood frames.
    *   **Recommended Thickness:** **18mm to 25mm (¾" to 1")**. Do not go thinner. An 18mm core is a good minimum. You can even laminate two 12mm sheets together for a 24mm frame, which will be exceptionally rigid.
*   **Alternative Recommendation: 6061-T6 Aluminum**
    *   **Why:** Stronger and more durable than wood. It allows for a slightly thinner profile while maintaining immense strength.
    *   **Recommended Thickness:** **10mm to 12mm (⅜" to ½")**. This would be practically indestructible for this application.

#### 2. Fork Width (Inside Gap)
For a long draw, you need a wider-than-average fork gap to prevent "band slap" against the frame and to provide a clear sight picture.

*   **Recommendation:** **10 cm (approx. 4 inches)**
*   **Rationale:** A standard slingshot might have an 8-9 cm gap. The wider 10 cm gap provides crucial clearance for the bands as they are stretched to a much more acute angle at full draw. It also helps reduce the torque effect on your wrist slightly compared to an even wider frame.

#### 3. Fork Height
This is the distance from the bottom of the fork "crotch" to the top of the fork tips. It needs to be tall enough to keep the drawn bands well clear of your gripping hand.

*   **Recommendation:** **4.5 cm (approx. 1.75 inches)**
*   **Rationale:** This height provides ample clearance to prevent the bands from ever touching your thumb or index finger, which is a major safety concern. The fork tips themselves should be about 2-2.5cm wide to provide a solid, durable surface for band attachment.

#### 4. Handle Dimensions
Ergonomics are key for a powerful setup. The handle should allow for a full, secure, and repeatable grip.

*   **Handle Length:** **12 cm (approx. 4.75 inches)**. This is long enough for a full-fist grip for an average adult hand, preventing any fingers from slipping off the bottom.
*   **Handle Shape & Thickness:** Instead of a simple rectangle, an ergonomic shape is far better.
    *   **Thickness (side to side):** **2.5 cm (1 inch)**
    *   **Width (front to back):** **3.5 - 4 cm (1.4 - 1.6 inches)** at its widest point (a "palm swell").
    *   **Design:** A "Coke bottle" shape with a swell in the middle for your palm and a flared base (pommel) is ideal. This locks the slingshot into your hand and prevents it from slipping under tension.

### Elastic Band Optimization

The goal here is to select a band configuration that can be stretched to your 76cm draw length while operating in its most efficient performance window.

#### 1. Material
*   **Recommendation:** **Flat Latex Sheeting**
*   **Why:** Flat bands are widely considered to be more efficient and produce higher projectile velocities than tubular bands of equivalent draw weight.
*   **Specifics:** Look for brands known for performance shooting, such as **Theraband Gold**, **Precise Latex**, or **GZK Latex**. These are consistent and well-regarded.

#### 2. Dimensions (Thickness and Taper)
*   **Thickness:** **0.8mm to 1.0mm**. This is a heavy-duty thickness appropriate for maximizing stored energy. A 0.8mm band will be slightly easier to draw and have a bit more speed, while a 1.0mm band will be a powerhouse for heavier projectiles.
*   **Taper:** **Crucial for performance.** A tapered band (wider at the forks, narrower at the pouch) is more efficient because it reduces the moving mass of the bands themselves.
    *   **Recommendation:** A **25-15mm taper**.
    *   **Meaning:** The band is 25mm wide at the end that attaches to the fork, and it tapers down to 15mm wide at the end that attaches to the projectile pouch. This is a powerful and popular taper.

#### 3. Active Length
This is the single most important calculation. It determines the stretch ratio of your bands, which dictates energy storage and band lifespan. The optimal stretch ratio for latex is typically between 500% and 600% (a factor of 5 to 6).

*   **Calculation:**
    *   **Stretch Ratio = Draw Length / Active Length**
    *   We can rearrange to find the Active Length:
    *   **Active Length = Draw Length / Desired Stretch Ratio**

*   **Recommendation:**
    *   Target a stretch ratio of **5.5** (or 550%). This is a perfect balance between power and not over-stressing the latex.
    *   Active Length = 76 cm / 5.5 = **13.8 cm**

Let's round that to a practical **14 cm (approx. 5.5 inches)** for the active length of your bands. This is the length of the latex *only*, not including the small amount used to tie it to the pouch and forks.
