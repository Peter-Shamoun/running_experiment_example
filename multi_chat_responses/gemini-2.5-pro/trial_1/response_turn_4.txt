### Answering Your Optimization Questions

#### 1. Can we use thicker bands (e.g., 1.2mm)?

**Yes, you can, and this is the primary method for increasing stored energy.** However, it comes with significant trade-offs that illustrate the law of diminishing returns:

*   **Pro:** Massively increases the force constant (`k`), leading to much higher stored Potential Energy (PE) at the same draw length.
*   **Con 1 (Human Factor):** The draw force increases dramatically. As we'll discuss, this quickly becomes the main limiting factor.
*   **Con 2 (Efficiency Loss):** A 1.2mm band is 20% thicker than a 1.0mm band, and thus 20% heavier. This added mass slows down the band's own contraction speed. A larger percentage of the stored energy is used just to move the bands themselves, meaning your **energy transfer efficiency (η) will decrease.**
*   **Con 3 (Safety & Usability):** The strain on the band attachment points and the torque on your wrist become extreme. Aiming becomes nearly impossible due to the immense effort required to hold the frame steady.

#### 2. Can we increase the stretch ratio (by decreasing active length)?

**This is a poor strategy for maximizing performance and a dangerous one for safety.**

*   **The Physics:** Latex stores energy very well up to a ~600% (6x) stretch ratio. Beyond this point, you gain very little additional stored energy, but you dramatically increase the internal stress on the material.
*   **The Reality:** Pushing bands to a 7x or 8x stretch ratio drastically shortens their lifespan—they might fail after just a few dozen shots. More importantly, hysteresis losses (energy lost to internal friction) increase at these extreme strains. The material becomes less "snappy."
*   **Conclusion:** The optimal range for both performance and reasonable longevity is **5.5x to 6.0x**. Deliberately going beyond this for a tiny potential energy gain is a bad trade-off. Your current 5.5x ratio is already in the high-performance sweet spot.

### Defining the Absolute Maximum Performance Ceiling

The true performance ceiling of *this specific system* is not limited by the frame's strength (18mm+ Baltic Birch can handle it) but by the **maximum force a human operator can safely and effectively manage.**

#### Limiting Factor: Human Draw Capability

For a slingshot held in the "pistol grip" style, even a very strong user will lose the ability to hold the frame steady and aim accurately long before they reach their absolute strength limit.

*   **Practical Limit for Accuracy:** ~20 kgf (45 lbs).
*   **Absolute Maximum for a Braced, Single Shot:** Let's set a generous theoretical limit at **25 kgf (approximately 55 lbs)**. Beyond this, control is lost, and the risk of catastrophic user error (like the frame twisting and causing a fork hit) becomes unacceptable.

This **25 kgf draw force is our primary constraint.** We will now design a bandset to meet this limit and calculate the resulting energy.

*   $KE_{max} = \eta_{max} \cdot PE_{max} = 0.75 \times 93.2 \text{ J}$
*   **$KE_{max}$ ≈ 69.9 Joules (Let's round to 70 J)**

**7. Calculate the "Ceiling" Muzzle Velocity:**
Using our new KE and projectile mass: $v = \sqrt{2KE/m}$
*   $v_{ceiling} = \sqrt{(2 \times 70 \text{ J}) / 0.0071 \text{ kg}} \approx \sqrt{19718}$

This heavier projectile, however, would retain its velocity far better over distance, likely resulting in a greater maximum range.
