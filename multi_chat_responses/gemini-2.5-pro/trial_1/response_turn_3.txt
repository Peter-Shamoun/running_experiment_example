### Part 1: Optimal Projectile Recommendation

To achieve maximum theoretical range, we must optimize the projectile's **Ballistic Coefficient (BC)**, which is its ability to overcome air resistance. The formula is $BC \propto \frac{m}{C_d A}$, where `m` is mass, `C_d` is the drag coefficient, and `A` is the frontal area.

This formula tells us we need a projectile that is **dense**, **small**, and **aerodynamically shaped**.

#### Material: Steel
*   **Why:** Steel is the ideal choice. It's very dense (~7.85 g/cm³), readily available in precise spherical shapes, non-toxic (unlike lead), and relatively inexpensive. For theoretical purposes, tungsten is denser, but steel is the practical optimum.

#### Shape and Diameter: 9.5mm (3/8 inch) Sphere
*   **Why a Sphere?** While a teardrop shape has a lower $C_d$, it's nearly impossible to launch consistently from a simple pouch without tumbling. A sphere provides a predictable $C_d$ (≈0.47) and is easy to center in the pouch for an accurate release.
*   **Why 9.5mm Diameter?** This is the "sweet spot" for such a powerful setup.
    *   **Too light (e.g., 8mm):** Would achieve a blistering initial velocity but would lose energy to drag very quickly, hurting its ultimate range.
    *   **Too heavy (e.g., 12mm):** Would have a lower initial velocity. While it would retain energy well, the initial launch speed might be too low to maximize the range equation.
    *   The 9.5mm ball provides a superb balance of mass (for momentum and energy retention) and a high initial velocity that the powerful bandset can generate.

#### Recommended Projectile Specifications:
*   **Material:** Steel
*   **Shape:** Sphere
*   **Diameter:** **9.5 mm (0.95 cm)**

### Part 2: Performance Calculations

Here we will calculate the energy storage, draw force, and kinetic energy output.

**Crucial Assumptions for Your Report:**
1.  **Linear Force-Draw Model:** We will model the latex bands as a perfect linear spring ($F=kx$). In reality, latex has a slightly progressive curve, but this is a very strong and standard approximation for theoretical analysis.
2.  **Energy Transfer Efficiency (η):** A Y-frame slingshot with flat bands is highly efficient. We will assume an efficiency of **η = 85%**. This accounts for energy lost to hysteresis, sound, and the kinetic energy of the bands/pouch themselves.

#### Calculation 1: Expected Draw Force (at 76cm)

The draw force of a tapered bandset is complex, but we can make a solid, empirically-based estimate.

*   **For 0.8mm Latex Bands (25-15mm taper):**
    *   This setup will produce a peak draw force of approximately **13.0 kgf (kilogram-force)**.
    *   $F_{max} = 13.0 \text{ kgf} \times 9.81 \text{ m/s}^2 \approx$ **127.5 Newtons**

*   **For 1.0mm Latex Bands (25-15mm taper):**

    *   $F_{max} = 16.5 \text{ kgf} \times 9.81 \text{ m/s}^2 \approx$ **161.9 Newtons**

#### Calculation 2: Theoretical Stored Potential Energy (PE)

Using our linear spring model, the stored energy is the area under the force-draw triangle. The formula is $PE = \frac{1}{2} F_{max} x$, where `x` is the draw length (0.76 m).

*   **For 0.8mm Latex Bands:**
    *   $PE = 0.5 \times 127.5 \text{ N} \times 0.76 \text{ m}$

*   **For 1.0mm Latex Bands:**

    *   **PE ≈ 61.5 Joules**

#### Calculation 3: Final Kinetic Energy (KE) of the Projectile

This is the stored potential energy multiplied by our efficiency factor ($KE = \eta \cdot PE$).
