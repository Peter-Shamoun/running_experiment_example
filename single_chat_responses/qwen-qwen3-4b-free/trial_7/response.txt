**Comprehensive Slingshot Design Proposal**  
*Objective: Construct a slingshot optimized for energy transfer, range, and precision, with quantitative validation for each design choice.*  

---

### **1. Frame Design**  
**Material:** Carbon fiber-reinforced polymer (CFRP) composite.  
**Dimensions:**  
- **Length:** 35 cm (optimized for balance between portability and stability).  
- **Width:** 5 cm (minimizes cross-sectional area for aerodynamic efficiency).  
- **Thickness:** 1 cm (ensures structural rigidity without excessive weight).  
**Mechanical Properties:**  
- **Young’s Modulus:** 150 GPa (CFRP’s high stiffness for energy storage).  
- **Density:** 1.5 g/cm³ (lightweight for portability).  
**Rationale:**  
- The CFRP frame minimizes mass while maximizing stiffness, reducing energy loss during recoil.  
- The 35 cm length allows for optimal limb placement (see below) to balance energy storage and projectile trajectory.  

---

### **2. Limb Design**  
**Material:** Synthetic elastomer (e.g., polyurethane with 0.1 MPa Young’s modulus).  
**Dimensions:**  
- **Length per limb:** 20 cm (standard for energy storage efficiency).  
- **Width:** 3 cm (ensures sufficient cross-sectional area for strain).  
- **Thickness:** 2 cm (controls flexibility and energy storage capacity).  
**Mechanical Properties:**  
- **Young’s Modulus (E):** 0.1 MPa (elasticity for energy storage).  
- **Strain Limit:** 30% (prevents over-stretching and material failure).  
**Rationale:**  
- The 20 cm length and 2 cm thickness maximize strain energy storage:  
  $$
  E_{\text{limb}} = \frac{1}{2} k x^2 \quad \text{where } k = \frac{E A}{L} = \frac{0.1 \times 10^6 \, \text{Pa} \times (0.02 \, \text{m} \times 0.03 \, \text{m})}{0.2 \, \text{m}} = 300 \, \text{N/m}
  $$  
  $$
  E_{\text{limb}} = \frac{1}{2} \times 300 \, \text{N/m} \times (0.1 \, \text{m})^2 = 1.5 \, \text{J}
  $$  
- The 30% strain limit ensures the limbs can store 1.5 J of energy without failure.  

---

### **3. Trigger Mechanism**  
**Design:** Metal spring-loaded trigger with a 10 cm stroke.  
**Mechanical Properties:**  
- **Spring Constant (k):** 300 N/m (as calculated above).  
- **Stroke (x):** 0.1 m (maximizes energy transfer to the projectile).  
**Rationale:**  
- The trigger ensures the limbs are stretched to 30% strain, transferring 1.5 J of energy to the projectile.  
- The spring’s stiffness (300 N/m) matches the limb’s modulus for seamless energy transfer.  

---

### **4. Projectile Design**  
**Material:** Carbon fiber-reinforced composite with a 10g mass.  
**Dimensions:**  
- **Length:** 5 cm (reduces drag).  
- **Diameter:** 2 cm (minimizes cross-sectional area for aerodynamic efficiency).  
**Mechanical Properties:**  
- **Mass (m):** 0.01 kg.  
- **Drag Coefficient (C_d):** 0.5 (smooth, streamlined shape).  
**Rationale:**  
- The 10g mass balances energy transfer (1.5 J) with precision:  
  $$
  v = \sqrt{\frac{2E}{m}} = \sqrt{\frac{2 \times 1.5}{0.01}} = 17.3 \, \text{m/s}
  $$  
- The projectile’s drag force at 17.3 m/s:  
  $$
  F_{\text{drag}} = \frac{1}{2} \rho v^2 C_d A = \frac{1}{2} \times 1.2 \, \text{kg/m}^3 \times (17.3)^2 \times 0.5 \times \pi (0.01)^2 \approx 0.0565 \, \text{N}
  $$  
- The drag force decelerates the projectile, but the 17.3 m/s velocity ensures a 30-meter range (assuming no air resistance).
