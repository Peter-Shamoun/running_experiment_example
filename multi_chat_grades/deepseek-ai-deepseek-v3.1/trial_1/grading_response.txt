### Core Engineering Principle: The Goal

The goal is to achieve the **maximum horizontal range (R)**. For a projectile launched from ground level (or near it), neglecting air resistance, this is given by:

**R = (v² * sin(2θ)) / g**

Where:
*   `v` = launch velocity
*   `θ` = launch angle
*   `g` = acceleration due to gravity (9.81 m/s²)

Since `g` is constant and `sin(2θ)` is maximized at **θ = 45°**, your primary controllable variables become:
1.  **Maximize Launch Velocity (`v`)**
2.  **Consistently achieve a Launch Angle of 45°**

Your entire design will revolve around maximizing the energy transferred to the projectile to achieve `v`, and then controlling the release mechanism to achieve the ideal `θ`.

---

### Recommended Design Approach: The Elastic Catapult

An elastic-based system (catapult/ballista) is an excellent choice due to its high potential energy density and relatively simple mechanics. The design philosophy should be: **Store as much elastic potential energy as efficiently as possible and transfer it to the projectile as abruptly and completely as possible.**

Let's break this down into subsystems.

#### 1. Energy Storage & Selection (The "Engine")

This is your most critical decision. The energy stored is the integral of force over distance (E = ∫ F dx).

*   **Principle:** Choose a material with a high **energy density** (energy stored per unit mass) and a suitable **force-distance profile**.
*   **Options:**
    *   **Latex Rubber Bands (The Classic):** High energy density, very low cost, but performance can vary with temperature and age.
    *   **Surgical Tubing:** More consistent and durable than standard rubber bands. Offers a good balance of force and stretch.
    *   **Bungee Cords:** Higher force potential, good for larger scales.
    *   **Elastic Polymers (e.g., TheraBand):** Excellent and highly consistent properties, often used in serious competitions.
*   **Design Tip:** Use many strands in parallel to increase the total force capability without increasing the stretch distance. This allows you to store more total energy (`E_elastic = 1/2 * k * x²`, where `k` is the effective spring constant).

#### 2. Energy Release Mechanism (The "Trigger")

A poor release mechanism will waste stored energy as friction, vibration, or delayed release.

*   **Principle:** The release must be **instantaneous** and **consistent**. It should not impede the arm's motion.
*   **Options:**
    *   **Spring-Loaded Pin:** A pin retracted by a solenoid or a strong spring mechanism.
    *   **Over-Center (Latch) Mechanism:** A mechanical latch that holds the arm under tension. When triggered, it rapidly releases. This is very common and effective.
    *   **Electromagnetic Release:** Using a powerful electromagnet to hold the arm. Cutting power provides a very clean release.
*   **Design Tip:** The release point should be precisely calibrated. Releasing the arm *before* it reaches its fully cocked position can sometimes allow for a more efficient "whipping" motion, transferring energy better than a static, fully stretched spring.

#### 3. Launch Arm & Leverage (The "Transmission")

The arm transmits the force from the elastic element to the projectile.

*   **Principle:** Maximize the **velocity at the tip** of the arm where the projectile is held. This is a function of torque and moment of inertia.
*   **Key Trade-off - Arm Length:**
    *   **Long Arm:** Provides a longer acceleration distance for the projectile, allowing it to reach a higher speed. However, it is heavier, which increases the moment of inertia and wastes energy accelerating the arm itself.
    *   **Short Arm:** Lighter and faster to accelerate, but the projectile's acceleration path is shorter.
*   **Optimization:** Find the **sweet spot** for arm length. Use a lightweight, rigid material (e.g., carbon fiber tube, aluminum, high-quality wood) to minimize mass while preventing flexing, which wastes energy. A **trebuchet-style design** (with a counterweight) is less common for elastic systems but is the ultimate example of optimizing leverage and should be studied for its principles.

#### 4. Projectile Guidance & Release (The "Control")

This ensures the 45° launch angle and a clean release.

*   **Principle:** The projectile must separate from the arm at the exact point where the arm's velocity vector is **45°** from the horizontal.
*   **Solution: A Guide Rail or Sling.**
    *   **Guide Rail:** Physically constrains the projectile to a specific angle. Very consistent and simple. Friction is the enemy here—minimize it.
    *   **Sling:** A pouch attached to the end of the arm. As the arm rotates, the sling unfurls, effectively lengthening the arm and increasing tip speed. It releases naturally when the forces align (a condition you can design for). This is often the highest-performance option as it allows a later, faster release. The release angle is determined by the length of the sling strings and the pouch design.

#### 5. Frame & Base (The "Structure")

*   **Principle:** **Stability and Rigidity**. All the energy must go to the projectile, not into shaking or moving the base.
*   **Design Tip:** Make the frame heavy and rigid. Use triangulation to prevent flex. The entire device should not "jump" forward upon launch. This is a common source of energy loss and inconsistency.

**Materials Needed:**
*   Your manufactured aluminum frame
*   **Spring scale or luggage scale** (rated for at least 120 lbf / 550 N)
*   **Strong cord** (e.g., paracord, nylon cord)
*   **Wooden dowel or a second fixed anchor point**
*   **Ruler or calipers** for precise measurement
*   **Permanent marker**

**Procedure:**

1.  **Establish a Baseline:** Before applying any load, use the ruler/calipers to meticulously measure and record the exact distance between the tips of the two forks. Mark this measurement point with the marker. Also, visually inspect the frame for any visible defects.

2.  **Create a Test Rig:**
    *   Securely fix the **handle** of your slingshot frame in a vise or clamp it firmly to a heavy, immovable workbench. Protect the finish of the handle with soft jaws or cardboard.
